Three directions for the portfolio

Each takes a different angle on "distinctive but professional." Pick one — or tell me what you'd combine.

A

The Operator — Dark, confident, number-led

Dark surface (charcoal/near-black) with a bold impact bar at the top showing your biggest numbers — 85% adoption, 30% faster go-live, €300K lift. Projects expand inline with P→A→O. Strong typographic contrast. Feels like: a senior PM who knows exactly what they're worth.

Dark palette Metrics-first High contrast
B

The Framework — Structure as identity ⭐ Recommended

Warm palette stays, but the site is architecturally built around Problem → Approach → Outcome. The hero section opens with a bold statement about how you work — not just who you are. Projects expand inline. A "How I think" section shows 3 sharp product principles. The structure IS the proof of out-of-the-box thinking.

Warm palette P→A→O throughout Principles section Bolder typography
C

The Editorial — Big type, strong opinions

Magazine feel. A large, bold opening statement in display type sets the tone immediately — something like "I find broken things and fix them." Numbered sections. Strong black/accent contrast against warm background. Projects expand inline. Feels like: someone who has a point of view and isn't afraid to say it.

Warm palette Display typography Opinion-led Numbered sections
Why B is recommended for a PM targeting startups
The signal it sends

Most PM portfolios list what they shipped. B shows how you think by making the structure itself a demonstration of your problem-solving framework. You're not claiming to think in first principles — the architecture proves it.

What A risks

Dark portfolios read as "designer or developer." A startup hiring manager for a PM role might unconsciously discount it. High reward if they love it, higher risk if they don't.

What C risks

Editorial needs strong writing to carry it — the words have to be as sharp as the design. If the copy doesn't land, the bold design amplifies the gap. Higher writing bar.
