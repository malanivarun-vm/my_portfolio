Blinkit · DSM Operational Hub

The data exists inside Blinkit's dark stores. The visibility does not.

DSM Operational Hub is a mobile command layer for Blinkit dark store managers during the 6–10 PM peak window. Built as part of a PM case competition, the product emerged from field research with one core finding: the person with the most decision authority on the floor has the least real-time information. This product closes that gap.

Case study · Live prototype Quick commerce Dark store ops Mobile-first
Problem

Peak-hour failure is not a staffing or inventory problem. It is an information asymmetry problem.

India's quick commerce model runs on a 10–15 minute delivery promise. During peak hours (6–10 PM), order volume spikes 30–50% above baseline. A high-demand Bengaluru store processes 1,200–2,000 orders per day — each with an internal budget of ~2.5–3 minutes for pick, pack, and handoff before the clock becomes the rider's problem.

Through field observation and interviews with dark store operations professionals, we identified two specific failure moments: pre-peak preparation failures caused by planogram mismatches (pickers sent to bins where items do not physically exist), and real-time blindness during the peak window itself. The Dark Store Manager — the only person with decision authority on the floor — had no live signal layer. Their tool showed aggregate counts, not causes, picker locations, or bottleneck types. When something went wrong, they found out when a rider complained at the counter. By then, the intervention window was gone.

Research

Primary research, not assumptions: 40% of peak hour spent physically gathering information that should be digital.

  • Field observation during a peak window — DSMs spend ~40% of the hour physically walking the floor to understand what is happening instead of acting on it.
  • Anonymous interviews with quick-commerce ops professionals confirmed that the existing Nexus/Partners dashboard is used primarily for post-hoc analysis, not real-time intervention.
  • Structured pain-point scoring across manager workflow stages. Real-time picker visibility scored a 3.7 importance-satisfaction gap (importance: 4.9, satisfaction: 1.2) — the highest of any pain point identified.
  • Secondary research: Blinkit operational model teardown, industry benchmarking across Zepto and Swiggy Instamart, and operational math validation (picker productivity, the 10-day learning curve for new hires, cascading cost of a single planogram mismatch).
Solution

Three modes, each mapped to a distinct phase of the DSM's day.

The product operates in three modes, not one monolithic dashboard. Each mode is designed around a specific decision context:

  • Prep Mode (4–6 PM): Planogram mismatch alerts surfaced before peak starts, so the picker never arrives at an empty bin at 7 PM. Staff attendance tracker and put-away completion status complete the pre-peak picture.
  • Ops Mode (6–10 PM): A three-tab command centre. Tab 1 shows active orders as cards coloured by SLA countdown (not order age — the critical design distinction). Each card shows the bottleneck type and surfaces a one-tap nudge to the picker. Tab 2 shows pending queue depth before it becomes active load — the number that tells the DSM the pressure coming before it arrives. Tab 3 shows picker workforce: experience level, current order, session performance vs. store average. The Dislodge button — Blinkit's kill switch that pauses incoming orders — is surfaced explicitly here with a confirmation gate rather than buried as an emergency measure.
  • Hand-Off Mode (end of shift): Auto-generated shift summary from session data. The next DSM walks in with context, not a verbal briefing with half the information lost.
Key decisions

Five decisions that shaped the product, each with an explicit trade-off.

  • Mobile, not tablet-mounted: The DSM moves. A tablet-mounted dashboard is a stationary tool. Designing for the device already in their pocket removes a hardware dependency — at the cost of tighter information density constraints.
  • SLA countdown, not order age: Order age is irrelevant. What matters is time remaining before a breach. Tying card colour to SLA countdown directs the DSM's attention to the most operationally critical order, not the oldest one. Trade-off: requires accurate SLA data from the backend — latency degrades the signal.
  • Template nudges, not freeform messages: At peak, a DSM cannot compose a message. Pre-mapped templates ("Check Bin D-03", "OOS confirmed — substitute approved") reduce the action to one tap. Trade-off: edge cases that do not fit a template still require out-of-band communication.
  • Dislodge surfaced explicitly: The kill switch already exists in Blinkit's system. Making it a visible, designed button (with a confirmation gate) acknowledges operational reality and ensures the DSM uses it consciously. Trade-off: surfacing the option may lower the psychological barrier to its use — mitigated by designing every other feature to make it unnecessary.
  • Shift handoff as first-class mode: Field research confirmed that post-shift context transfer is almost entirely verbal, with significant information loss. An auto-generated summary costs nothing to produce from session data and eliminates a known reliability gap.
